Capacitor#

Introduction#

Electrical capacitance represents the ability of a component to store energy in the form of an electric charge. It is measured in farads (F) and defined as the ratio between the stored charge on the plates of a capacitor and the voltage applied across them. The main device that exhibits this property is the capacitor itself.

Basic Principle#

A capacitor consists of two conductive plates separated by an insulating material known as the dielectric. When a voltage is applied between the plates, positive charges accumulate on one plate and negative charges on the other, creating an electric field across the dielectric.

This ability to store and release energy rapidly makes capacitors essential in many electronic systems, such as power supplies, signal filters, and timing circuits.

Fundamental Equation in Electronics#

Current–Voltage Relationship#

The defining equation of a capacitor in the time domain is:

\[ i(t) = C \frac{du(t)}{dt} \]

This means that the current through the capacitor depends on the rate of change of the voltage across it. In other words, a faster voltage variation leads to a larger current. A sudden change in voltage can therefore produce a high inrush current, which must be considered during circuit design.

Capacitor Impedance#

The complex impedance of a capacitor is expressed as:

\[ Z_C = \frac{1}{j \omega C} = -\frac{j}{\omega C} = \frac{1}{\omega C} e^{-j \frac{\pi}{2}} \]

Several important observations can be made:

  • The impedance is inversely proportional to frequency: – at high frequencies, the capacitor behaves like a short circuit (it passes AC easily); – at low frequencies, it behaves like an open circuit (it blocks DC).

In summary:

Frequency

Impedance Behavior

\(f \to \infty\)

\(Z_C \to 0\) (short circuit)

\(f \to 0\)

\(Z_C \to \infty\) (open circuit)
